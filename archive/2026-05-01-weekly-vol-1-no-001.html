CHAPTER I · WEEK IN REVIEW · 28 APR – 1 MAY 2026

FOUR DAYS THAT
Rewrote THE TAPE.

The week opened Monday with a haymaker: the Wall Street Journal reported OpenAI missed its own Q1 revenue and user targets — ChatGPT targeting 1B weekly active users reached only ~900M, with market share in generative AI web traffic falling from 80%+ to 64.5%. AI infrastructure names (NVDA, AVGO, ORCL, AMD) all fell 1–3% and the Nasdaq dropped −1.1% on the session. Tuesday brought Powell's final press conference as Fed Chair — rates held at 3.5–3.75% on an 8-4 vote, the most dissents since October 1992 — while Kevin Warsh cleared the Senate Banking Committee the same morning. Wednesday was the week's centerpiece: GDP came in at +2.0% (below estimates), PCE inflation exploded to +4.5% from +2.9%, and after the bell Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft all reported. GOOGL surged on 63% Cloud growth; Meta and Microsoft got hammered on $125–190B capex raises. Thursday brought Apple's blowout close — EPS $2.01 vs. $1.96 expected, revenue $111.2B (+17% YoY), Greater China +28%. Then Friday, an Iran peace signal via Pakistani mediators triggered a sharp oil relief rally. The SPX closed at ~7,254, above all-time highs, and the week was done.

CHAPTER III · MACRO · GDP + PCE + FOMC · 29–30 APR 2026

THE POLICY
Bind.

Wednesday's BEA advance estimate handed the Fed the worst possible combination: Q1 GDP printed +2.0% annualized (rebounding from Q4 2025's near-stall at +0.5%, but still below the +2.3% expected), while Q1 PCE inflation surged to +4.5% — up from +2.9% in Q4. Core PCE hit +4.3%. The culprit is tariff pass-through now fully embedded in consumer baskets. The Fed, which held rates at 3.5–3.75% on Tuesday in an 8-4 vote, has no easy escape: cutting would pour gasoline on an already-hot inflation print; holding further risks cracking a growth picture already showing cracks. The most dissents since October 1992 reflect that internal tension exactly. Powell's four-shock framing — pandemic, Ukraine, tariffs, Iran — is accurate but doesn't resolve the bind. Kevin Warsh, who advances to the chair role on May 15, inherits a stagflation setup the Fed hasn't faced in 40 years. His first meeting is June. Watch for whether June brings a hawkish reset or a capitulation to political pressure on rate cuts.

CHAPTER IV · GEOPOLITICS & THE WEEK'S BOOKEND · APR 30 – MAY 1

THE STRAIT
And THE CLOSE.

The oil disruption was the macro overhang of the week. US-Israel military action against Iran created a siege of Iranian ports, threatening the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint handling ~35% of global seaborne crude. The IEA characterized the resulting ~10M bbl/day supply reduction as "the largest supply disruption in history." Brent crude briefly surged to $126/bbl intraday Wednesday, April 30, the highest print since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. Oil price risk was a direct PCE inflation amplifier, reinforcing the Fed's stagflation bind. Then Friday changed the tone: Iran sent a response to a US peace proposal through Pakistani mediators. Oil pulled back sharply, energy stocks faded, and equities popped — with Apple providing the clean bullish catalyst. AAPL Q2 (fiscal) reported after Wednesday's close: EPS $2.01 beat $1.96 estimates; revenue $111.2B (+17% YoY) beat $109.66B; Greater China +28%. Q3 guidance came in above consensus. The iPhone cycle is alive, China headwinds reversed, and AAPL closed the week as the cleanest large-cap long on the tape.

CHAPTER VI · WHAT WORKED THIS WEEK

THE Winners' EDGE.

No formal setups were flagged this week — the Brief's first daily issue publishes Monday. But the market delivered clear verdicts on directional theses that were live entering the week. Here's what paid.

GOOGL LONG — +10.0% · CLOUD CONVICTION
THESIS: Cloud acceleration, buyback expansion
The thesis was simple: Google Cloud's growth rate was accelerating into a generative AI adoption cycle, and Alphabet's buyback program (announced $70B in Q1) provided a floor. Q1 cloud revenue hit $20B, +63% YoY. The market had priced in ~50% growth — the beat was material. Total revenue +22% YoY. Stock hit all-time highs. What worked: the focus on revenue delivery over capex spending ratio. GOOGL's capex-to-revenue ratio is lower than META/MSFT; it reads as more efficient AI infrastructure deployment.
AAPL LONG — +4.6% · CHINA RECOVERY + IPHONE CYCLE
THESIS: China headwinds peaking, new iPhone cycle
Greater China revenue $20.5B, +28% YoY — the bull case was that China regulatory/macro headwinds had peaked and a new iPhone AI feature cycle would re-accelerate. Both played out. Revenue +17% YoY, EPS +22%. Q3 guidance above consensus. The lesson: AAPL's China exposure, which the market had been discounting for 6 months, turned from headwind to tailwind in one quarter. Better to buy the pessimism trough than chase the beat.
LONG OIL — BRENT +19% MO · GEOPOLITICAL PREMIUM
THESIS: Iran conflict → Hormuz disruption risk
Anyone positioned long energy / long Brent crude heading into April captured ~19% in oil prices during the month. The risk was obvious from March's escalation signals. The IEA confirmed ~10M bbl/day disrupted — the largest in history. Brent hit $126 intraday Wednesday. The Friday Iran peace signal reversed gains — but the directional trade, held through Wednesday, was the clearest macro setup of the month.

CHAPTER VII · WHAT DIDN'T WORK THIS WEEK

THE Wrong SIDE.

Honest accounting. Three distinct trade frameworks got punished this week — each with a logic that seemed defensible entering the week but failed for specific, identifiable reasons.

META LONG — −9.0% · CAPEX STICKER SHOCK
THESIS: AI monetization compounding → revenue growth
The revenue beat was real ($56.3B, +$860M vs. estimates, EPS $7.31 beat by $0.52). The thesis wasn't wrong on the revenue side. Where it broke: the market in 2026 is explicitly pricing capex multiples, not just revenue multiples. Raising the 2026 capex guide to $125–145B (from $115–135B) with no specific monetization timeline attached to the incremental spend was the tell. JP Morgan's downgrade to Neutral with a $725 PT cut landed within hours. Lesson: even a revenue beat can't overcome a capex guide that doesn't show near-term ROI.
MSFT LONG — −4.0% · THE AI DOUBT TAX
THESIS: Azure momentum + Copilot monetization
Microsoft beat on every meaningful line: EPS $4.27 (+$0.21 beat), revenue $82.89B (+$1.5B beat), Azure growth confirmed. The thesis was right. The stock sold off anyway. The market imposed an "AI doubt tax" — ~$190B annualized capex without clear near-term return specifics created the same dynamic as META. Owning a clean beat going into earnings was not enough. The lesson: in a capex-skeptical market, even quality beats get punished if the forward spend guide is large and open-ended.
AI INFRASTRUCTURE LONG — −1 TO −3% · OPENAI MISS SIGNAL
THESIS: AI infra demand infinite → NVDA/AVGO/ORCL re-rate
Monday's WSJ report — OpenAI missed its own Q1 revenue and user targets, with generative AI web market share falling from 80% to 64.5% — compressed the "infinite AI demand" narrative that had been driving NVDA, AVGO, ORCL, and AMD to extended valuations. Each fell 1–3% on Monday. The underlying demand thesis may still be intact long-term, but the marginal story for 2026 is no longer "infinite." This creates multiple compression risk for names trading at AI-premium valuations.
